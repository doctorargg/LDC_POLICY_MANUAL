Medication Reconciliation Protocols

Critical Safety Process

Accurate medication reconciliation prevents adverse events and ensures therapeutic optimization. This process requires meticulous attention to detail.

Three-Source Verification Method:

  • Patient Report: What the patient says they're taking
  • Pharmacy Records: What's been filled in last 12 months
  • Previous Provider Records: What was prescribed

Reconciliation Components:

  • Prescription Medications: Name, dose, frequency, indication, prescriber
  • Over-the-Counter Products: Including occasional use items
  • Supplements & Vitamins: Brand, dose, frequency, reason for use
  • Herbal Products: Traditional medicines, teas, tinctures
  • Discontinued Medications: Recent stops and reason for discontinuation
  • Adherence Assessment: Honest discussion about medication-taking patterns

Wisconsin-Specific Requirements & Compliance

State-Specific Compliance

All patient journey processes must comply with Wisconsin state regulations, including specific requirements for DPC practices.

DPC-Specific Wisconsin Requirements

  • Membership Agreement: Must meet all statutory requirements per Wis. Stat. § 609.80
  • Fee Transparency: All fees must be clearly disclosed in writing before enrollment
  • Insurance Coordination: Cannot bill insurance for services covered by DPC fee
  • Termination Rights: Both parties can terminate with 30 days written notice
  • Non-Discrimination: Cannot refuse or terminate based on health status

Wisconsin Privacy & Records Requirements

Beyond HIPAA Requirements

Wisconsin has additional privacy protections that exceed federal HIPAA requirements.

  • Mental Health Records: Special protections under Wis. Stat. § 51.30
  • HIV/AIDS Information: Specific consent required for disclosure
  • Substance Abuse Records: Federal 42 CFR Part 2 plus state requirements
  • Minor Consent: Special rules for reproductive health, mental health, and substance abuse treatment
  • Record Retention: Minimum 5 years after last entry (longer for minors)

Mandatory Reporting Requirements

Legal Reporting Obligations

Healthcare providers in Wisconsin have specific mandatory reporting requirements that must be integrated into patient care processes.

  • Child Abuse/Neglect: Immediate report to county department or law enforcement
  • Elder Abuse: Report to county adult protective services within 48 hours
  • Communicable Diseases: Report to local health department per state requirements
  • Violent Injuries: Gunshot wounds, stabbings must be reported to law enforcement
  • Impaired Drivers: May report to DMV under specific circumstances

Regulatory Requirements & Compliance

Legal Requirement

All controlled substance records must be maintained for a minimum of 5 years from the date of record and be available for inspection by authorized persons (DEA, state authorities).

Federal Requirements (DEA)

  • DEA Registration: Current DEA registration required for all controlled substance handling
  • Schedule Classification: Ketamine is Schedule III (moderate abuse potential, accepted medical use)
  • Biennial Inventory: Complete inventory of all controlled substances every two years
  • Perpetual Inventory: Ongoing record of all receipts, administration, and disposal
  • Security Requirements: Secure storage in locked cabinet or safe

Wisconsin State Requirements (Med 17.05)

  • Separate Bound Log Book: Each schedule of controlled substances must be recorded separately
  • Chronological Order: All entries must be in chronological order
  • Patient Records: All controlled substances dispensed must also be recorded in patient record
  • Location Requirement: Records maintained at location where substances are received/dispensed

Required Information for Each Entry

Wisconsin law requires the following information for each controlled substance administration:

  • Name of the substance
  • Dosage form and strength of the substance
  • Name and address of the person for whom dispensed/administered
  • Date of dispensing/administration
  • Quantity dispensed/administered
  • Name or initials of practitioner who dispensed/administered

⚡ Common Medical Emergencies Quick Reference

Anaphylaxis

  1. 1. Call 911 immediately
  2. 2. Administer epinephrine (0.3-0.5mg IM)
  3. 3. Position patient lying flat, elevate legs
  4. 4. Give oxygen if available
  5. 5. Prepare second dose of epinephrine
  6. 6. Monitor vitals continuously

Cardiac Arrest

  1. 1. Call 911 and get AED
  2. 2. Begin chest compressions immediately
  3. 3. Push hard and fast (100-120/min)
  4. 4. Apply AED as soon as available
  5. 5. Continue CPR between shocks
  6. 6. Switch compressors every 2 minutes

Seizure

  1. 1. Protect from injury (move objects)
  2. 2. Do NOT restrain or put anything in mouth
  3. 3. Turn on side if possible
  4. 4. Time the seizure duration
  5. 5. Call 911 if >5 minutes or first seizure
  6. 6. Monitor breathing, stay with patient

Syncope/Fainting

  1. 1. Lower patient to floor safely
  2. 2. Elevate legs 12 inches
  3. 3. Check pulse and breathing
  4. 4. Loosen tight clothing
  5. 5. Keep patient lying for 10-15 min
  6. 6. Vital signs before allowing to sit/stand

Progressive Discipline Policy

Fair and Consistent Accountability

Our progressive discipline policy ensures fair treatment while maintaining high standards for patient care and professional conduct.

Disciplinary Framework

Level 1: Verbal Coaching

  • First minor infraction or performance issue
  • Documented in employee file with date and details
  • Focus on education and correction
  • No formal disciplinary action
  • Follow-up within 30 days to ensure improvement

Level 2: Written Warning

  • Repeated minor infractions or first moderate infraction
  • Formal documentation required with specific expectations
  • Clear timeline for improvement (typically 30 days)
  • Employee acknowledgment signature required
  • Copy provided to employee and HR file

Level 3: Final Written Warning

  • Continued performance issues or serious infraction
  • May include suspension (paid or unpaid)
  • Performance Improvement Plan required
  • Clear statement that termination may result
  • HR and leadership review required

Level 4: Termination

  • Failure to improve after final warning
  • Severe policy violation
  • HR and leadership approval required
  • Documentation package prepared
  • Exit procedures initiated immediately

📚 Evidence Hierarchy & Evaluation

GRADE Evidence Quality Assessment

A
High Quality Evidence

Multiple well-designed RCTs, systematic reviews with consistent results

Implement with confidence
B
Moderate Quality Evidence

Limited RCTs, well-designed cohort studies, some inconsistency

Implement with monitoring
C
Low Quality Evidence

Case-control studies, poor quality cohorts, expert opinion

Consider carefully, track outcomes
D
Very Low Quality Evidence

Case reports, anecdotal evidence, conflicting results

Use only if no alternatives
