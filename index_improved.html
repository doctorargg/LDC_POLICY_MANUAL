WI DPC Membership Agreement Checklist

Per Wisconsin law, all Direct Primary Care agreements must meet the following criteria to be valid. This checklist ensures our patient membership agreement is compliant.

  • In Writing & Signed: The agreement must be written and signed by both the practice and the patient (or their legal representative).
  • Termination Clause: Must allow either party to terminate with written notice, and provider termination must follow specific state rules (e.g., cannot be based solely on health status).
  • Services Described: Must describe and quantify the specific primary care services covered by the fee.
  • Fees & Duration Specified: Must clearly state the subscription fee, payment terms, and agreement duration.
  • No Third-Party Billing: Must prohibit billing an insurer for services covered by the fee.
  • Required Disclosures: Must prominently state in writing:
    • The agreement is NOT health insurance.
    • It may not satisfy federal coverage requirements on its own.
    • The patient is responsible for non-covered service fees.
    • Patient is encouraged to consult an insurance advisor.
    • Fees might not apply to insurance deductibles or out-of-pocket maximums.

Scheduling & Appointment Management

Our scheduling system prioritizes patient access and convenience, offering same-day/next-day appointments for urgent needs and extended appointment times.

  • Appointment Types: Clearly defined time allocations (e.g., New Patient: 60-90 min, Follow-up: 30 min, Urgent: 20 min).
  • Same-Day Access: Reserved slots in the schedule daily to accommodate urgent issues. Triage protocols determine medical urgency.
  • Cancellations: Require 24-hour notice to avoid potential fees. Policy is communicated clearly to all patients.
  • Confirmations: Automated reminders sent 24-48 hours prior via patient's preferred method (text, email, portal).

Communication Systems

Effective and secure communication is a cornerstone of our DPC model. All communications must be professional, timely, and protect patient privacy.

Secure Messaging Protocol

  • Primary Channel: The patient portal is the primary method for secure clinical communication.
  • Response Time: Urgent messages responded to within 4 business hours; routine messages within 24 business hours. This expectation is set with patients during onboarding.
  • Appropriate Use: Not for emergencies. Complex issues require a telehealth or in-person visit.
  • Documentation: All clinically significant messages are documented in the patient's EHR.

Telephone & Other Channels

  • After-Hours: Urgent calls are routed to the on-call provider. The outgoing message clearly directs patients to call 911 for true emergencies.
  • Email/Text: Standard email/text is for non-clinical communication only (e.g., appointment reminders). Any PHI must be sent via encrypted email or the secure portal.
  • Multilingual Support: Direct communication in Spanish is available. Professional interpreter services are used for other languages to ensure clear understanding, especially for informed consent.

Clinical Operations & Workflows

This section details the protocols for delivering high-quality, evidence-based patient care, including standards for documentation and procedures for our specialized services. These workflows are designed to leverage the strengths of the DPC model for comprehensive health management.

Patient Care Protocols

Clinical Assessment & Evaluation

Every assessment is comprehensive, utilizing active listening and the extended time afforded by the DPC model to perform thorough histories and physical exams.

Chronic Disease Management

Leverage the DPC model for proactive, coordinated care. Protocols focus on patient education, self-management support, lifestyle modification, and regular follow-up to optimize outcomes for conditions like diabetes, hypertension, and mental health.

Controlled Substance Management (Wisconsin Specific)

  • ePDMP Checks Required: A check of the Wisconsin electronic Prescription Drug Monitoring Program (ePDMP) is mandatory before issuing a prescription for any monitored drug (Schedules II-V).
  • Chronic Pain: Prioritize non-opioid therapies. If opioids are necessary, aim for <50 MME/day and document justification clearly for exceeding this threshold.
  • Telehealth Prescribing: Requires at least one in-person evaluation within the past 12 months for controlled substances.

Physician Dispensing (Wisconsin Specific)

Physicians in Wisconsin may dispense medications from the office. If this service is offered, Lotus Direct Care must adhere to all regulations, including:

  • Proper Labeling: Must include practice name/address, date, patient name, drug name/strength, directions, and cautionary statements.
  • Record-Keeping: Meticulous records of all dispensed medications must be kept.
  • ePDMP Reporting: All dispensed controlled substances must be reported to the ePDMP by 11:59 p.m. the next business day.
  • Secure Storage: Medications, especially controlled substances, must be stored securely according to DEA regulations.

Clinical Documentation Standards

"If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done." Documentation must be complete, accurate, and timely to ensure care continuity and compliance.

  • Standard: Use SOAP note format (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan) in the EHR for all encounters.
  • Timeliness: All encounters must be documented within 24 hours.
  • Record Retention: Per Wisconsin law, medical records must be retained for a minimum of five years after the last patient entry.
  • Communications: Document all clinically significant patient communications (portal messages, phone calls) in the medical record.
  • Specialized Services: Documentation for functional medicine, mental health, or addiction treatment must be especially detailed, including rationale for non-conventional tests, specific informed consent discussions, and clear treatment plans.

Specialized Service Protocols

Protocols for our unique service offerings, ensuring care is evidence-based, compliant, and integrated.

Functional Medicine

Informed Consent is CRITICAL

For any non-conventional tests or treatments, a detailed informed consent discussion is mandatory. Document the rationale, evidence base (or lack thereof), potential risks, benefits, alternatives, and costs. Ensure the patient understands and consents explicitly.

  • Assessment: Use detailed intake forms to capture extensive history on diet, lifestyle, environment, and stress.
  • Advanced Labs: Document clear rationale for specialized testing (e.g., organic acids, stool analysis).
  • Treatment Plan: Detail specific nutritional interventions, supplement recommendations (with dosages and rationale), and lifestyle changes. Monitor for interactions and outcomes.

Mental Health Services

Heightened Confidentiality (Wis. Stat. ยง 51.30)

Mental health and substance use records have stricter confidentiality protections under Wisconsin law than general medical records. Be vigilant about disclosures.

  • Screening: Use standardized tools like PHQ-9 (depression) and GAD-7 (anxiety).
  • Crisis Intervention: Follow protocols for assessing and managing crises, including safety assessment and timely referral to emergency services. Document all actions.
  • Documentation: Psychotherapy notes, if provided, are kept separate and have specific consent requirements for release.

Addiction Treatment

Federal (42 CFR Part 2) & State Confidentiality

Records identifying a patient as receiving SUD treatment are protected by stringent federal and state laws. Unauthorized disclosure has severe penalties. A specific, written consent is required for almost any disclosure.

  • Assessment: Use comprehensive tools (e.g., ASAM criteria) to assess addiction severity and guide treatment planning.
  • Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): If provided, requires specific informed consent, strict adherence to prescribing regulations (including ePDMP), and regular monitoring for efficacy and diversion.
  • Relapse Prevention: Collaboratively develop a written relapse prevention plan with the patient, identifying triggers and coping strategies.

Ketamine Assisted Psychotherapy Protocols

Comprehensive KRF (Ketamine Research Foundation) evidence-based protocols specifically tailored for Lotus Direct Care's ketamine assisted psychotherapy program, emphasizing integration, safety, and therapeutic outcomes.

Lotus Direct Care KAP Philosophy & Approach

Our Commitment to KRF Standards

Lotus Direct Care follows the Ketamine Research Foundation (KRF) guidelines for comprehensive Ketamine Assisted Psychotherapy (KAP), emphasizing therapeutic relationship, integration, and patient safety over simple medication administration.

Core Principles

  • Psychotherapy Integration: Ketamine is administered as part of comprehensive psychotherapy, not merely as a drug treatment
  • Therapeutic Relationship: Emphasis on warmth, attention, and openness - the most important factors in psychotherapeutic success
  • Experience Processing: Every ketamine experience deserves to be heard, shared, and processed through Integration sessions
  • Harm Reduction: Comprehensive support and monitoring to prevent misuse and maximize therapeutic benefit
  • Personal Familiarity: Dr. Rosenberg has personal experience with ketamine to better understand and relate to patient experiences

Differentiation from "Drug-Only" Approaches

Lotus Direct Care KAP vs. Traditional Ketamine Clinics:

  • Higher Success Rates: KAP approach shows significantly higher success than drug-only (~30% remission) approaches
  • Integration Focus: Processing experiences vs. just medication administration
  • Extended Sessions: 2-3 hours vs. brief infusion appointments
  • Therapeutic Presence: Continuous support vs. minimal interaction
  • Comprehensive Care: Complete assessment and follow-up vs. limited evaluation

Comprehensive substance use history including current use patterns, previous dependencies, and recovery history. Active substance abuse is an absolute contraindication.

  • Historical Patterns: Previous substance use disorders and treatment

Appropriate Candidates for Lotus Direct Care KAP

  • PTSD unresponsive to conventional treatments
  • Anxiety disorders with treatment resistance
  • Individuals seeking personal growth and healing
  • Patients able to engage in psychotherapy process

Session Environment & Setting

Three-Phase KAP Session Structure

Phase 1: Opening Period (30-45 minutes)

  • Connection Building: Establish therapeutic presence and rapport
  • Current State Review: Assess current stresses, concerns, and mental state
  • Session Framing: Set intentions and expectations for the experience
  • Preparation: Final safety checks and medication preparation
  • Relaxation: Guided relaxation and centering exercises

Phase 2: Ketamine Experience (60-90 minutes)

  • Administration: Careful ketamine administration via chosen route
  • Supportive Presence: Continuous therapeutic presence and monitoring
  • Non-Directive Support: Allowing natural unfolding of experience
  • Safety Monitoring: Vital signs and psychological state monitoring
  • Gentle Guidance: Minimal intervention, maximum support

Phase 3: Integration (45-60 minutes)

  • Experience Processing: Sharing and exploring what was experienced
  • Meaning Making: Discussing impact, insights, and significance
  • Difficulty Processing: Addressing any challenging aspects
  • Transition Planning: Preparing for return to daily life
  • Follow-up Planning: Scheduling integration sessions

Administration Routes & Protocols

Sublingual/Buccal (Primary Method)

  • Lozenges/RDTs: Rapid dissolving tablets or troches
  • Proper Technique: "Swishing" saliva for 15-20 minutes
  • Absorption: Through mucosal lining, not swallowing
  • Onset: 15-30 minutes, duration 60-90 minutes

Intramuscular (When Indicated)

  • Rapid Onset: 2-3 minutes to effect
  • Predictable Absorption: More reliable than oral routes
  • Multiple Doses: 2-3 injections may be used
  • Duration: 45-75 minutes peak experience

Controlled Substance Logging Protocols

Comprehensive protocols for the proper logging, inventory management, and regulatory compliance for controlled substances administered in the office, including ketamine and other Schedule III substances.

Regulatory Requirements & Compliance

Legal Requirement

All controlled substance records must be maintained for a minimum of 5 years from the date of record and be available for inspection by authorized persons (DEA, state authorities).

Federal Requirements (DEA)

  • DEA Registration: Current DEA registration required for all controlled substance handling
  • Schedule Classification: Ketamine is Schedule III (moderate abuse potential, accepted medical use)
  • Biennial Inventory: Complete inventory of all controlled substances every two years
  • Perpetual Inventory: Ongoing record of all receipts, administration, and disposal
  • Security Requirements: Secure storage in locked cabinet or safe

Wisconsin State Requirements (Med 17.05)

  • Separate Bound Log Book: Each schedule of controlled substances must be recorded separately
  • Chronological Order: All entries must be in chronological order
  • Patient Records: All controlled substances dispensed must also be recorded in patient record
  • Location Requirement: Records maintained at location where substances are received/dispensed

Required Information for Each Entry

Wisconsin law requires the following information for each controlled substance administration:

  • Name of the substance
  • Dosage form and strength of the substance
  • Name and address of the person for whom dispensed/administered
  • Date of dispensing/administration
  • Quantity dispensed/administered
  • Name or initials of practitioner who dispensed/administered

Daily Logging Procedures

Pre-Administration Protocol

1

Inventory Verification

Verify current inventory count matches log book records. Investigate and document any discrepancies immediately.

2

Patient Verification

Confirm patient identity using two identifiers. Verify prescription/order authorization and dosage.

3

Pre-Administration Documentation

Document starting inventory amount and prepare administration record entry.

Administration Documentation

Required Log Book Entry Format:

Date/Time: MM/DD/YYYY, HH:MM

Substance: Ketamine HCl

Strength: 50 mg/mL

Amount: [quantity administered]

Patient: [Full name and address]

Practitioner: [Name or initials]

Witness: [If required]

Balance: [Remaining inventory]

Post-Administration Protocol

1

Waste Documentation

Document any unused portions or waste. Witness disposal if required. Update inventory balance.

2

Patient Record Entry

Enter administration details in patient's medical record, including dose, time, route, and patient response.

3

Security Check

Ensure controlled substances are properly secured and storage area is locked.

Inventory Management & Security

Storage Requirements

  • Secure Cabinet: DEA-approved locked cabinet or safe, bolted to floor/wall
  • Limited Access: Only authorized personnel have keys/combinations
  • Separate Storage: Controlled substances stored separately from other medications
  • Environmental Controls: Appropriate temperature and humidity control
  • Inventory Segregation: Each schedule stored and tracked separately

Perpetual Inventory System

Daily Balance Verification

At the end of each day, verify that the physical inventory matches the log book balance. Investigate any discrepancies immediately.

  • Opening Balance: Record starting inventory each day
  • Receipts: Log all new controlled substance deliveries
  • Administrations: Record each use with running balance
  • Waste/Loss: Document any waste, spillage, or loss
  • Closing Balance: Verify ending inventory matches calculations

Biennial Inventory Requirements

  • Frequency: Complete physical inventory every two years (at minimum)
  • Documentation: Record exact quantities of each controlled substance
  • Opened Containers: Estimate quantities in opened vials/containers
  • Reconciliation: Compare physical count to perpetual inventory records
  • Discrepancy Investigation: Document and investigate any variances

Loss or Theft Reporting

Immediate Action Required

  • Report to DEA within 1 business day (DEA Form 106)
  • Report to local police immediately
  • Document circumstances and investigation
  • Implement additional security measures as needed

Disposal & Waste Management

Waste Documentation Requirements

  • Immediate Documentation: Record waste at time of occurrence
  • Witness Requirement: Second person witness for waste disposal when possible
  • Reason Documentation: Clear reason for waste (unused portion, contamination, etc.)
  • Method Documentation: How waste was disposed of or destroyed
  • Signature Requirements: Both disposing practitioner and witness signatures

Approved Disposal Methods

Small Quantities (Daily Waste)

  • Witness disposal down sink/toilet
  • Document in waste log immediately
  • Both parties sign waste entry
  • Update perpetual inventory

Large Quantities (Expired Stock)

  • DEA authorized disposal method
  • Licensed waste disposal company
  • DEA Form 41 for destruction
  • Certificate of destruction required

Record Retention

5-Year Retention Requirement

All controlled substance records, including disposal documentation, must be retained for minimum 5 years and be readily available for inspection.

  • Daily administration logs
  • Waste and disposal records
  • Biennial inventory records
  • Purchase invoices and receipts
  • DEA forms and correspondence
  • Loss/theft reports and investigations

Practice Operations & Safety

This section covers the essential operational and safety protocols that protect our staff, our patients, and our practice. It includes key OSHA requirements, state-specific rules for facility management, and procedures for laboratory testing.

OSHA: Workplace Safety

We are committed to providing a safe work environment by complying with all OSHA standards.

Bloodborne Pathogen (BBP) Exposure Control Plan

This is a mandatory written plan that must be reviewed and updated annually. Key components include:

  • Exposure Determination: Identifying jobs and tasks with potential exposure.
  • Compliance Methods: Use of universal precautions, engineering controls (e.g., sharps containers), and work practice controls (e.g., no recapping needles).
  • Hepatitis B Vaccination: Offered free to at-risk employees.
  • Post-Exposure Follow-up: A clear, confidential protocol for evaluating and treating any exposure incidents.
  • Annual Training: Mandatory for all staff with potential exposure.

Hazard Communication

  • Safety Data Sheets (SDSs): Must be readily accessible for every hazardous chemical in the practice.
  • Labeling: All chemical containers must be properly labeled.
  • Training: Staff must be trained on the risks of chemicals they use.

Medical Waste Disposal (WI DNR Rules)

Proper handling and disposal of medical and infectious waste is governed by the Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources (DNR) and is critical for public and environmental health.

1

Segregation

Separate infectious waste (e.g., sharps, items saturated with blood) from regular trash at the point of generation.

2

Containment & Labeling

Sharps go in rigid, puncture-resistant, labeled containers. Other infectious waste goes in sealed, leak-proof bags within rigid, labeled outer containers.

3

Storage

Store in a designated, secure area for no more than 90 days before disposal.

4

Transport & Disposal

Must use a licensed commercial medical waste hauler. Do NOT use local residential drop-off sites.

5

Record-Keeping

Maintain waste manifests and certificates of destruction for at least five years.

CLIA: Laboratory Testing

Any on-site lab testing, even simple "waived" tests, must comply with the Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (CLIA).

  • Certificate of Waiver: The practice must maintain a current CLIA Certificate of Waiver covering all tests performed on-site.
  • Manufacturer's Instructions: We must strictly follow the manufacturer's instructions for every test, without deviation.
  • Quality Control (QC): Perform and document QC tests as required by the manufacturer (e.g., daily for many point-of-care tests). Patient testing cannot proceed if QC fails.
  • Training & Competency: All personnel performing tests must be trained and have their competency assessed and documented annually.

HIPAA: Patient Privacy & Security

Protects all Protected Health Information (PHI). Key requirements include the Privacy Rule, Security Rule, and Breach Notification Rule. We must enforce the "Minimum Necessary" standard, secure patient authorizations, and have robust Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) with all vendors handling PHI.

OSHA: Workplace Safety

Ensures a safe environment for all staff. Requires a written Bloodborne Pathogen Exposure Control Plan (updated annually), a Hazard Communication program (with accessible SDSs), provision of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), and mandatory safety training.

CLIA: Laboratory Standards

Governs all on-site lab testing. For waived tests, we must maintain a Certificate of Waiver, follow all manufacturer instructions precisely, perform and document quality controls, and ensure all testing personnel are trained and competent.

WI Medical Examining Board (MEB)

Governs the practice of medicine in Wisconsin. Key rules include standards of professional conduct (Med 10), telehealth requirements (Med 24), record-keeping (5-year retention), and truthful advertising. All care must meet the standard of minimally competent medical practice.

WI DPC & Patient Rights Law

State laws provide specific rules for DPC agreements to distinguish them from insurance. They also grant patients specific rights, especially for mental health/SUD records (Wis. Stat. ยง 51.61) and minor consent, which may be stricter than HIPAA.

WI DNR: Environmental Safety

The Department of Natural Resources sets stringent rules for medical and infectious waste disposal. This includes proper segregation, containment, labeling, storage, and use of a licensed commercial hauler. Manifests must be kept for 5 years.
